From Educational Segregation to Computational Health Geography

Research Philosophy

My research journey represents an evolution from methodological expertise to purposeful application. Starting with educational segregation analysis, I discovered that spatial accessibility is the common thread connecting education, transport, environment, and health inequalities. This realization drives my current focus on computational health geography, where I develop innovative methods to understand how place, identity, and social outcomes intersect.

Core Mission: Using advanced computational methods and AI/ML to reveal and address spatial inequalities, with particular focus on intersectional analysis through MAIHDA (Multilevel Analysis of Individual Heterogeneity and Discriminatory Accuracy).

MAIHDA Innovation Agenda

Developing next-generation intersectionality methods for social science

Causal-MAIHDA

Integrating causal inference methods (DID, RDD, IV) with MAIHDA to move from description to causation.

ML-MAIHDA

Using machine learning for automated feature discovery and ensembles to uncover hidden intersections.

Spatial-MAIHDA

Geographic random effects to understand how place modifies identity effects.

LLM-MAIHDA

LLMs for narrative generation and discovering new intersectional categories from text data.

Real-time MAIHDA

Streaming applications for continuous monitoring of intersectional inequalities.

Open Tools

R packages and interactive web apps to democratise MAIHDA methods.

Completed Research

PhD Thesis: Ethnic School Segregation in England

Completed 2023

Developed multi-group, multi-scale frameworks to understand segregation patterns in English schools over 20 years. Findings revealed overall declines in residential and school segregation with regional variation, establishing the foundation for accessibility-focused work.

Key Contributions:

  • Entropy-based multi-group segregation measures
  • Identified London’s unique school–residence correlation pattern
  • Published in Population, Space and Place (JCR Q1)
  • Methodological basis for later transport/accessibility research
